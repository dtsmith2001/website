Programming

Every quantitative analyst on Wall Street eventually has to implement a model to price or risk manage a position or portfolio. The skill sets are usually Excel/VBA, C++, C#, SQL, and perhaps a scripting language such as Perl or Python. Don't ignore R/S-Plus and Mathematica/Maple/Matlab. I'm going to focus on C++ here as the others are relatively easier to learn. For the basics, see

Modeling and Data

Most of your time will be spent dealing with data quality and cleaning datasets. This is the single most important step of the modeling process, and may account for over 90% of the time you spend developing a model. Unmodeled effects can also introduce uncertainties into forecasts. For an example of this, see the JPL paper summarizing prediction and modeling of the orbit of the asteroid Apophis.

Data series can have unexpected changes, which may change the results of models in unpredictible ways. For instance, US government data series are often adjusted, which means announced results of GDP, inflation, etc may not be reflected in the actual data series. Fortunately, the St. Louis Fed maintains "clean" data series. The Fred database is useful for some studies, but historical data may be adjusted months after the intial release. If you want the data series exactly the way it was announced, use the Alfred database.

Join the R Finance list and search for data vendors or sources of data in the archives to find information about high-frequency stock, commodity, or forex data.

Using a more sophisticated statistical model, such as the GLM or robust estimators, is not the answer to known or suspected data problems. And if you want a bit of perspective on regression models, see Freedman's article Statistical Models and Shoe Leather. I would recommend always scaling your data and use regularization. There are also ways to check your model using data you withhold from the estimation step. This can be used to do determine the "correct" order of polynomial terms in a regression, etc. And then you can use a separate testing data set to make sure your model works out-of-sample. If you want to learn more about this, take Andrew Ng's Coursera class Machine Learning.

If you have already studied econometrics and factor models (see the Fama-French three-factor model and "A Better Three Factor Model that Explains More Anomalies"), there are investment banks and hedge funds that run portfolios using such models. They typically use earnings, leverage, and other financial information to create indicators to use in regression models. See Ernie Chan's blog post for some further information.

Don't forget to apply late days to quizzes and assignments if you get behind. Time management is important to avoid getting overwhelmed.

Please be aware that quite a lot of work has appeared in the past few years related to the use of partial differential equations and lattice/finite difference methods to price securities such as mortgage-backed securities. This method avoids the use of separately estimated prepayment and default models, as the boundary conditions of the PDE problem incorporate the optionality in the mortgage contract. The Libor Market Model is usually implemented using Monte Carlo, but simulations can be very slow indeed. A few papers on the subject are

  • Sharp, N.J., D.P. Newton, and P.W. Duck, "An Improved Fixed-Rate Mortgage Valuation Methodology With Interacting Prepayment and Default Options" Journal of Real Estate Finance and Economics, 2008, volume 36, issue 3, p. 307-342. A good overview is http://www.ricam.oeaw.ac.at/conferences/fayr07/Talks/Sharp.pdf

  • An Efficient Lattice Algorithm for the Libor Market Model, Tim Xiao.

Daniel Duffy recommends against using the Crank-Nicholson method for solving partial differential equations. It is known to have problems when calculating the second derivatives numerically, which is required for risk management purposes. See

  • Robust and Accurate Finite Difference Methods in Option Pricing One Factor Models

  • Performance and Accuracy Analysis of Integration Schemes for the Black-Scholes Equation

Duffy's site Datasim Financial has other papers of interest as well.

For another look at an interesting technique, go over to the Mathworks web page and view the free webinar "Cointegration and Pairs Trading with Econometrics Toolbox".

Finding A Job

There are two types of firm, buy-side and sell-side. Sell-side firms include investment banks, and broker-dealers. Anyone else is a buy-side firm. Here are links to the relevant Wikipedia pages.

  • Investment Bank

  • Broker-dealer

  • Pension Fund

  • Mutual Fund

  • Hedge Fund

Use eFinancialCareers.com. Many recruiters, including company recruiters, search this web site, so be sure to post your resume.

Set up alerts at the job sites run by SIAM, AMS, the CFA Institute, and eFinancialCareers.

You will find math jobs posted on Dice.com, however the site is directed at IT. It's easy to get trapped into a IT role, which you will find hard to leave. Sometimes it's difficult to avoid this, especially if you have bills to pay and really need a position. In this case, try to minimize its effect on your resume. Don't stay too long in such a position and keep up your knowledge in quantitative analysis by reading the literature and developing models in your spare time. Publishing a paper is a good way to distinguish yourself and indicate interest in the subject.

If you have already studied econometrics and factor models, talk to a recruiter about this. Long/short equity hedge funds often use these types of models.

You can always use the contacts via my LinkedIn Profile. There are quite a few recruiters among my contacts. Send them an email and let them know you are looking for a job as a quantitative analyst.

Learning About the Financial Markets

One way to learn about the financial markets is to take the Level 1 CFA exam. For further information, see the CFA Program web site. Try to finish the exams early in your career. Having the CFA charter is essential to getting a job on the buy side.

Two sites I try to read regularly are David Merkel's Alephblog and John Hussman's weekly commentary. Both have skin in the game, as it were, as they actively invest on behalf of others. Note that Hussman's Strategic Growth Fund is a long-time holding of mine. To get you started, Merkel maintains a list of "best" articles, and Hussman discusses how he hedges his stock holdings against market risk.

Quantitative Analysis Books

Here is a biased list of books on quantitative analysis.

  • Options, Futures, and Other Derivatives. John Hull's book is a good introduction to the subject. It has chapters on every topic you will encounter on Wall Street. It's where I start when researching a topic I've not encountered before or need a refresher.

  • Financial Calculus: An Introduction to Derivative Pricing. Works thru the discrete time case before introducing the complications of continuous time. I found I understood the Radon-Nikodym Theorem much better after reading this book, and better understood the importance of equivalent measures. My measure theory class in grad school didn't do a good job with these two topics. I found Folland's Measure Theory book to be much better than Royden or Rudin. Folland has two chapters on topology that are really very good, as well as good chapters on Fourier Analysis, distributions, Sobolev spaces, and partial differential equations.

  • Stochastic Differential Equations: An Introduction with Applications. The theory behind the Black-Scholes model for options pricing.

  • Quant Jobs Questions and Answers. To get a flavor of the questions you may be asked on an interview.

  • Monte Carlo Methods in Finance.

  • Paul Wilmott on Quantitative Finance

  • Quantitative Trading: How to Build Your Own Algorithmic Trading Business.

  • Evidence-Based Technical Analysis: Applying the Scientific Method and Statistical Inference to Trading Signals. Technical analysis is highly prone to confirmation bias. This book is a welcome breath of fresh air.

  • The Econometrics of Financial Markets.

  • A Non-Random Walk Down Wall Street.

As always, make sure you get the latest editions.
